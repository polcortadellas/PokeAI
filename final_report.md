# 🧠 PokeAI: Training a Reinforcement Learning Agent to Play Pokémon Red

Welcome to **PokeAI**! This is my personal project where I built and trained an autonomous Reinforcement Learning (RL) agent to play the classic Game Boy game *Pokémon Red*. My main goal was to see if I could teach an AI to navigate a complex, non-linear RPG environment using the **Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)** algorithm.

## 🛠️ Tech Stack
* **Language:** Python 3.12
* **Reinforcement Learning:** Stable-Baselines3 (PPO Algorithm)
* **Environment Wrapper:** Gymnasium
* **Emulation:** PyBoy (Headless & SDL2 Visual Mode)
* **Monitoring & Metrics:** TensorBoard & My Custom CLI PowerShell Pipeline

---

## 🎯 My Reward Strategy

In Reinforcement Learning, the agent learns entirely through a custom reward system. Balancing these numbers was the hardest part of my coding process. I quickly learned that if you reward the wrong behavior, the AI will ruthlessly exploit it.

This is the final, highly-tuned `RewardConfig` I created to push my agent forward:

* **Map Discovery (+1000.0):** The ultimate goal. I rewarded this massively to force the agent to travel and find new cities instead of staying in one place.
* **Badges (+5000.0):** The grand milestones of the game.
* **Pokédex Registration (+50.0):** Encourages catching new Pokémon.
* **Exploration (+2.0):** Small dopamine hits for walking on unseen tiles.
* **Battle Victory (+10.0) & Level Up (+2.0):** I nerfed these significantly in my later runs to prevent the agent from "farming" low-level enemies infinitely.
* **Step Penalty (-0.001) & Menu Penalty (-0.1):** Punishes the agent for wasting time or endlessly clicking through menus (unless it's in a battle).

## 🚀 My Journey: Evolution of the Agent (Run by Run)

Training this agent was not a straight line. My AI constantly found hilarious and unexpected ways to "hack" my math. Here is the chronological evolution of my model:

### Phase 1: The House Prisoner
Initially, my agent struggled to even leave the starting house. It would walk into walls or endlessly open and close the start menu. By tweaking the exploration rewards and adding a strict UI menu penalty, I finally taught it to walk out the front door.

### Phase 2: The Cowardly Hacker
Once outside, the agent faced wild Pokémon. However, it realized that battles open text menus, and menus penalize its score. The agent's brilliant solution? **Become a pacifist and run away from every single battle.**
* **The RAM Glitch:** Even without fighting, my agent somehow reported reaching **Level 177** and scoring 11,500 points. I dug into the memory and discovered a glitch: during the unskippable Professor Oak tutorial, the Game Boy RAM shuffles data around to load graphics, briefly pushing garbage data into the "Party Level" memory address. My AI learned to intentionally trigger the Oak cutscene just to cash in on this fake level-up reward!

### Phase 3: The VIP State & The Warrior
To fix the tutorial RAM glitch and the agent's cowardice, I completely changed my approach:
1. **The VIP Save State:** I manually played through the tedious tutorial myself, bought 5 PokéBalls in Ciudad Verde, and saved the emulator state right in front of the tall grass. The agent now spawns ready for war.
2. **Time Limit Expansion:** I realized 2048 steps was only ~13 minutes of playtime, making the agent panic. I bumped its lifespan to **20,480 steps**, giving it plenty of time to explore and fight.
3. **Reward Rebalance:** I turned off menu penalties during combat.

### Phase 4: Reaching Viridian City (The Breakthrough!)
With the new rules in place, I moved the project to a CUDA-enabled GPU, forcing it to run at a blazing **2000+ FPS**. In just under 5 Million timesteps, my agent achieved its greatest milestone yet. It stopped running, started fighting, threw PokéBalls, caught new Pokémon, and successfully navigated Route 1 to reach **Viridian City** entirely on its own!

## 📊 Demystifying the PPO Logs (How to Read My Metrics)

If you look at my terminal logs or my TensorBoard metrics, you will see a table generated by Stable-Baselines3. Here is my breakdown of what these numbers actually mean for the agent's brain:

### `rollout/` (The Gameplay Metrics)
* **`ep_rew_mean` (Episode Reward Mean):** This is the most important number I watch. It shows the average score the agent achieves per run. When this goes up, I know my AI is getting smarter.
* **`ep_len_mean` (Episode Length Mean):** How long the agent survives before resetting. When this hits my max limit (20,480), it means my agent is surviving until time runs out without hitting a game-over screen or getting stuck in a loop.

### `train/` (The Brain's Math)
* **`entropy_loss`:** This measures how "random" or "curious" the AI is. It starts high (trying random buttons) and slowly drops as the agent becomes confident in its strategy. If it drops too fast, the agent gets stuck doing one repetitive action forever (Local Optima).
* **`value_loss`:** The agent tries to predict how many points it will get in the future. This measures how wrong its predictions were. A lower value means my AI accurately understands my reward system.
* **`policy_gradient_loss`:** Indicates how much the "actor" (the part of the brain choosing the buttons) is changing its behavior to optimize its paths.
* **`explained_variance`:** A score between `0` and `1`. It tells me how well the AI's internal predictions match reality. If it approaches `1.0`, it means my AI understands exactly what will happen to the game state when it presses a button.
